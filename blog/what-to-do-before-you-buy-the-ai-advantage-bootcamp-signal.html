The Smart Checklist for What to Do Before Buying AI Advantage

Breaking Out June 26, 2026

The $995 Question Nobody Stops to Ask

You just finished a webinar. Maybe it was live, maybe it was a replay you watched at midnight with a cup of tea going cold beside you. Tony Robbins was intense. Dean Graziosi was sharp. The examples felt real and the transformation arc felt possible. Now there is a payment page with your cursor hovering over it, and something in your gut is saying do it, do it, do it.

Here is the checklist you actually need before that finger clicks. Not because AI Advantage is a trap. It probably isn't. But because a thousand dollars deserves more than ninety minutes of inspired feeling, and you deserve to know the difference between a genuine fit and a well-engineered yes.

Step One: Name Your Actual Problem Out Loud

Grab a piece of paper, not a note on your phone. Write the sentence: "Right now, the thing eating my time or money that AI could fix is..." and then finish it in plain, specific language.

If you write "I want to understand AI better," that's not a problem. That's a mood. Dig one layer deeper.

  • I spend six hours every week writing emails I hate writing.
  • I can't afford a graphic designer but I need visuals every single week.
  • I have a product and zero system for answering customer questions at scale.
  • I record voice memos full of ideas that never become anything.

That's a problem worth buying a solution for. That's a problem you can test, measure, and track. "Understanding AI better" is a feeling that a course will temporarily satisfy before the next shiny thing shows up. Specific problems have specific answers, and knowing yours tells you whether this particular bootcamp is actually the answer.

Step Two: Give Free Tools One Honest Week

Here's the part most people skip entirely, and it's the most useful thing on this list. Before you buy training on AI tools, use AI tools. For free. For seven days. No shortcuts.

Open ChatGPT or Claude or Gemini, bring it the specific problem you wrote down in step one, and actually work with it every day for a week. Don't just play. Work. Bring real tasks. Write a real email. Generate a real caption. Ask it to help draft a real client proposal.

At the end of seven days, one of two things will be true. Either you'll realize you've already solved 60 percent of your problem on your own, using free tools and your own curiosity, which means a structured bootcamp is optional rather than urgent. Or you'll hit clear, specific walls, you'll know exactly where you're stuck, and you'll be able to walk into any course purchase knowing precisely what you need it to do for you.

Both outcomes are genuinely valuable. The first one saves you a thousand dollars. The second one tells you what to look for when evaluating whether AI Advantage specifically addresses your gaps. Either way, you win.

Step Three: Separate the Structure from the Knowledge

Most AI courses, including this one, aren't primarily selling you secret information. The information is largely available. What they're selling is structure, sequencing, accountability, and community. Those are real things with real value, but they're only worth what you'll actually use.

Ask yourself these three questions honestly:

  1. Do I have a track record of finishing online courses?
  2. Do I actually engage with community spaces, or do I join them and go silent?
  3. Am I stuck on knowledge gaps, or am I stuck on consistency and follow-through?

If your honest answers are "not really," "not really," and "follow-through," then paying for structure makes complete sense. Structure is the product. Go get it. But if you're someone who learns fast on their own, who watches courses at 2x speed and applies them immediately, you might be paying for accountability you won't use.

Neither answer makes you a better or worse person. They just tell you different things about what you actually need right now.

Step Four: Clock the Urgency and Decide Who It Belongs To

The pressure you feel on that checkout page was built intentionally. Countdown timers, bonus stacks that expire, the implied idea that everyone else is already ahead of you and you're falling further behind by the minute. These are real techniques used by real experts in persuasion, and they work because they're aimed at the part of you that hates missing out.

Here's a clean test. Close the tab. Wait 24 hours. Come back tomorrow and see if the offer is still there.

If the bonus disappeared exactly at midnight, you just learned something about how that company relates to your decision-making process. Real value doesn't actually evaporate by morning. Offers structured that way are designed to prevent you from thinking, not to reward you for acting fast. If the course is still there tomorrow with essentially the same terms, that's a healthier sign, and you can make your decision from a calmer place.

Step Five: Read What Real Buyers Are Saying in the Messy Places

Don't just read the testimonials on the sales page. Those were selected. Read the comments on YouTube videos reviewing the course. Check Reddit threads where people talk about it weeks after buying, not the day after. Look for the pattern of what people got out of it and where they felt the gap between expectation and delivery.

You're not looking for reasons to cancel the purchase. You're looking for honest signal about whether the experience matches your situation. Someone saying "this was amazing if you're starting from zero" is useful information if you're not starting from zero. Someone saying "the community is where the real value is" tells you something important about how the course actually works versus how it's marketed.

Step Six: Match the Offer to Your Specific Lane

AI Advantage is a broad-spectrum bootcamp built for a wide audience. That's its strength and also its limitation. If your situation is specific, a general course might give you 40 percent of what you need and 60 percent of content that doesn't apply to your life.

Before buying, look for targeted resources that speak directly to your situation. A musician needs different AI tools than a coach. A freelance writer needs different workflows than a physical product seller. Broad training has its place, but it pairs best with knowing your specific angle going in.

Worth reading side by side with this one before you decide anything.

The Bottom Line on Buying Smart

None of this is an argument against buying AI Advantage. It might be exactly the right move for you. Structured learning with accountability and community is genuinely valuable, especially if you've struggled to build AI habits on your own.

But the goal is a clean decision, not a reactive one. The goal is knowing why you're buying, what problem you're solving, and whether this specific product is the actual tool for that problem or just the loudest thing in front of you this week.

Run the checklist. Spend the week with free tools. Write down the problem. Check the real reviews. Give yourself the 24-hour test. Then decide.

A decision made that way is one you can stand behind, whether you buy or whether you walk. That's the whole point.
